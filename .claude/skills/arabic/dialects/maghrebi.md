# 🇲🇦🇩🇿🇹🇳 مغربي — Maghrebi Arabic (Darija)

**Countries:** Morocco (~37M), Algeria (~46M), Tunisia (~12M)
**Note:** Each country has a distinct dialect. Moroccan Darija is the most widely studied. Cover all three.
**When to use:** North Africa targeted content, Moroccan market specifically, European Arab diaspora (France, Spain, Belgium, Netherlands)

---

## 1. Country Variations

### مغربي — دارجة (Moroccan Darija)
The most globally recognized Maghrebi dialect due to Moroccan diaspora size (France, Spain, Belgium, Italy, Netherlands).
- Heaviest French influence
- Most distinct from MSA — nearly incomprehensible to Eastern Arabs
- Casablanca: urban prestige. Marrakech: tourist-aware. Fes: traditional/classical.
- Young Moroccan audience: highly digital, French/Arabic/Spanish trilingual

### جزائري (Algerian Arabic / Derdja)
- Heavy French influence, similar to Moroccan
- Some Amazigh (Berber) vocabulary mixed in
- Oran vs Algiers accent variation
- Also very distinct from Eastern Arabic
- Large diaspora in France

### تونسي (Tunisian Arabic / Tounsi)
- Between Masri and Moroccan in comprehensibility to Eastern Arabs
- Some Italian loanwords (historical)
- French influence, but less than Morocco/Algeria
- Most open politically — 2011 Arab Spring birthplace = liberal reputation
- Tunis urban vs. interior regional variation

**Key principle:** A piece of Moroccan Darija content will NOT be understood by Saudi Arabians. These are genuinely different languages in practice, not just accents.

## 2. Phonology — Sound Rules

### Moroccan Darija
| Feature | Darija | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ق (qaf) | often silent or q | varies by region |
| Vowel reduction | MAJOR — short vowels often dropped | "كتب" → "ktb" / "عندي" → "'ndi" |
| French R | uvular r from French adopted | |
| ث/ذ | t/d (simplification) | |
| Cluster consonants | Very common — initial clusters | "بغيت" → "bɣit" / "كلامك" → "klamk" |

**Most distinctive feature:** Moroccan Darija drops short vowels heavily, creating consonant clusters unusual in other Arabic dialects.

### Tunisian Arabic
- More vowels preserved than Moroccan
- Closer to Eastern Arabic phonology
- Italian loanwords: "forchetta" → فورشيتة

## 4. Grammar Patterns

### Moroccan Darija present continuous
- كاين + verb: "كاين يكتب" = there is / he is writing
- راه + present: "راه يكتب" = he's writing (state description)
- بـ prefix also used: "بيكتب" (borrowed from Masri in some urban areas)

### Future tense
- غادي + verb: "غادي نكتب" = I'm going to write (Moroccan)
- باش + verb: "باش نكتب" = in order to write (can also indicate near future intent)

### Negation
- ما + verb + ش: "ما كتبتش" = I didn't write (Moroccan: double sandwich like Egyptian)
- "والو" for "there's nothing / no"
- "مانيش" = I'm not (Tunisian: pronoun + negation)

## 5. Register Scale

| Level | Description | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| L1 — Rural/Regional | Village dialect | Heritage, traditional brands |
| L2 — Sha'bi | Working class urban | Mass market consumer |
| L3 — Urban Darija | Casablanca/Rabat standard | Most commercial content |
| L4 — Mixed Darija-French | Professional urban youth | Tech, startup, lifestyle |
| L5 — MSA or French | Formal official | Legal, government, education |

**Note:** In Morocco, formal content often appears in French (not MSA). For serious B2B: French is often preferred over MSA.

## 7. Brand Voice Guide

### Consumer brands (Morocco)
- Warmth through family and hospitality: Moroccan hospitality culture (مائدة / كرم الضيافة) is central
- Code-switching is natural — don't fear mixing French
- Authenticity: Moroccan audience detects imported content easily
- Humor: Moroccan humor is self-aware and gentle; dark irony
- Local pride: Casablanca, Marrakech, the Atlas, the medina — these are identity anchors

### Moroccan youth brands (18–30)
- Heavy L4 — Darija/French/English mix
- Very digitally sophisticated
- International pop culture fluency alongside strong local identity
- "يلاه نبدأو" energy — forward-looking, not nostalgic

### Diaspora content (Moroccan/Algerian in France)
- Dual identity tension is real — acknowledge both sides
- Ramadan content especially resonates — connects to both home and identity
- French code-switching is appropriate and natural
- Second-generation diaspora: often more comfortable in French but emotionally connected to Arabic

### Tunisian brands
- More politically explicit in content (post-2011 openness)
- Humor: more direct and dry than Moroccan
- Female autonomy: Tunisia has strongest women's rights tradition in Arab world — messaging can reflect this
- B2B: often French-first, Arabic secondary

## 8. Cultural Context

### Amazigh (Berber) co-identity
Morocco and Algeria have significant Amazigh populations. For content in these regions:
- Amazigh pride is growing — avoid erasing Amazigh identity in Moroccan content
- Using a Tamazight word occasionally signals respect (أزول = hello in Tamazight)
- Never frame Moroccan identity as purely Arabic

### French colonial legacy
- French is a co-official or widely-used professional language across all three countries
- For premium brands: French signals sophistication to Maghrebi audiences
- For mass market: Darija / Arabic signals authenticity and connection

### Religious context
- Morocco: Maliki Islam, generally moderate
- Algeria: similar to Morocco
- Tunisia: most secular Arab Muslim country
- Ramadan observance: very strong in all three countries
- Halal compliance: assumed for food brands

## 10. Cross-Dialect Contamination — Avoid These

### In Moroccan Darija — avoid:
| Phrase | Dialect it belongs to | Replace with |
|---|---|---|
| أيوه | Masri | إيه / صافي / آه |
| إيه | Masri (what) | آش / اشنو |
| طب | Masri transition | يلاه / باهي |
| بجد | Masri | بجد (acceptable but less natural) → والله |
| هيك | Levantine | هكذا / هادشي |
| شو | Levantine | آش |
| بصراحة (Gulf opener) | Khaliji | بصراحة (acceptable) — but flatten the Gulf tone |
| وايد | Khaliji | بزاف |
| هواية | Iraqi | بزاف |
| أكو/ماكو | Iraqi | كاين/ما كاينش |
